# Product Framing Guidance

Product framing defines the problem before the project sprints heroically toward solving the wrong one.

## Purpose

Every serious project should explain:

- what problem exists
- who has the problem
- why the problem matters
- what happens if nothing changes
- what success looks like
- what is out of scope
- what assumptions and risks exist

## Minimum Expectations

At minimum, capture:

1. Problem statement
2. Target users or roles
3. Current pain or opportunity
4. Desired outcome
5. Success criteria
6. Known risks and assumptions
7. Security/privacy/abuse notes
8. Explicit non-goals

## Full Product Framing Checklist

### 1. Problem Statement

Define the problem in plain language.

Capture:

- current situation
- affected users/roles
- pain points
- frequency/severity
- business or mission impact
- why now

Useful questions:

- Who is suffering?
- What are they trying to do?
- What fails today?
- What evidence do we have?

### 2. Opportunity and Goals

Describe the opportunity and intended value.

Capture:

- project goals
- user success criteria
- business/project success metrics
- SEO/discovery goals for public web projects
- strategic value
- constraints
- tradeoffs

### 3. Users and Stakeholders

Identify everyone affected.

Capture:

- primary users
- secondary users
- admins/operators
- approvers/reviewers
- support users
- security/privacy stakeholders
- enterprise buyers/procurement reviewers, where relevant
- external auditors or regulators, where relevant

### 4. Context and Existing Alternatives

Understand what exists now.

Capture:

- current workflow
- current tools
- manual workarounds
- competitor/comparable products
- search/discovery alternatives for web projects
- known frustrations
- migration or adoption constraints

### 5. Security, Privacy, and Abuse Framing

Identify risk early.

Capture:

- sensitive data involved
- likely abuse cases
- trust boundaries
- external exposure
- user permissions/roles
- regulatory/compliance concerns
- enterprise compliance expectations such as SOC 2 attestation, ISO/IEC 27001 certification/alignment, vendor-risk review, or security questionnaires
- consequences of misuse or failure

### 6. Operational Framing

Consider operation from day one.

Capture:

- who operates this
- expected number of users/projects/environments
- routine maintenance needs
- automation opportunities
- support expectations
- uptime/recovery expectations

### 7. Assumptions and Unknowns

List what is not yet proven.

Capture:

- product assumptions
- technical assumptions
- security assumptions
- market/user assumptions
- operational assumptions
- research needed

### 8. Non-Goals and Boundaries

Define what this project is not.

Capture:

- excluded users
- excluded workflows
- excluded platforms
- excluded integrations
- future possibilities intentionally deferred

## Lightweight Project Version

For small projects:

1. One-paragraph problem statement
2. Target user/role list
3. Top 3 goals
4. Top 3 risks/assumptions
5. Security/privacy note
6. Out-of-scope list

## Definition of Framing-Ready

A project is framing-ready when:

- the problem is clear
- users and stakeholders are named
- success criteria exist
- major risks and assumptions are known
- security/privacy/abuse concerns are visible
- non-goals are explicit

## Definition of Framing-Done

Product framing is done when the relevant human agrees this is the right problem to pursue next.
